# Evidence: Subject Lines & Opening Hooks

**Dimensions:** 1 (Subject Lines), 2 (Opening Hooks & First Lines)
**Date:** 2026-02-06
**Sources:** Gong/30MPC (85M emails), Lavender AI, Belkins (5.5M emails), Woodpecker (20M emails), Digital Bloom 2025, Kyle Coleman, Becc Holland, Josh Braun, Florin Tatulea

## Findings

### Finding: Shorter subject lines (1-4 words) dramatically outperform longer ones
**Confidence:** CONFIRMED
**Evidence:** Lavender.ai, Gong/30MPC, Belkins 2025 — accessed 2026-02-06

- Lavender: 2-word subject lines get 60% more opens than 5-word. Going from 2 to 4 words reduces replies by 17.5%.
- Gong/30MPC (85M emails): Keep under 4 words. Shorter = higher open rate.
- Belkins (5.5M emails): Open rates drop beyond 7 words (39%), further after 9 words (35%), 10-word at 34%.
- Woodpecker: Subject lines up to 70 characters had 43.38% open rate; 240+ chars dropped to 23.5%.

**Implications:** Write 1-4 word subject lines. Two words is optimal per Lavender data.

---

### Finding: The "Internal Camo" method — subject lines should look like internal emails, not sales pitches
**Confidence:** CONFIRMED
**Evidence:** Lavender.ai (Will Allred), Kyle Coleman, Florin Tatulea — accessed 2026-02-06

- Lavender: Subject should look like a to-do list item — short, descriptive, boring. Should feel like it came from inside the company.
- Avoid: verb-driven language, superlatives, "you"-focused language, emojis, anything that fits outside vendor email patterns.
- Examples: "Reply Rates", "Trial Delays", "Hiring Ops", "2024 Forecasts"
- Florin Tatulea: Name target contact's direct reports in subject line to show insider knowledge.

**Implications:** Subject lines describe the email's topic neutrally. Avoid sales-sounding language.

---

### Finding: First names in subject lines HURT replies; numbers and punctuation reduce opens
**Confidence:** CONFIRMED
**Evidence:** Lavender.ai — accessed 2026-02-06

- First names in subject: 12% fewer replies (signals automation)
- Numbers in subject: 46.33% less likely to get opened
- Punctuation in subject: 36% less likely to get opened
- Kyle Coleman: "Stop it with the first name tokens."

**Implications:** Never put prospect's first name in subject. Avoid numbers and punctuation entirely. Personalization belongs in the body/first line.

---

### Finding: Casing data is CONFLICTING between major sources
**Confidence:** INFERRED (conflicting data)
**Evidence:** Lavender.ai, Gong/30MPC, Belkins 2025 — accessed 2026-02-06

- Lavender: "Not using title case can reduce opens by 30%." Recommends title case.
- Gong/30MPC (85M emails): "All lowercase subject lines have highest open rates."
- Belkins (5.5M emails): ALL CAPS at 30% open rate; title case and lowercase both at 29%.
- Independent A/B test: All-lowercase got 35% more opens/clicks vs headline-style.

**Implications:** Both title case and lowercase outperform sentence case. This is a prime A/B test candidate — right answer likely depends on ICP/industry.

---

### Finding: Question-based subject lines achieve 46-48% open rates
**Confidence:** CONFIRMED
**Evidence:** Belkins (5.5M emails), Woodpecker, Focus Digital — accessed 2026-02-06

- Belkins: Questions average 46% open rate, outperforming all other types.
- Cold emails with questions: average 48.39% open rate.
- Woodpecker: Questions boost opens by 10%.
- Tension with Lavender's "no punctuation" finding — question marks may be slight negative signal.

**Implications:** Questions work via curiosity gaps. Consider question-framed subjects without the question mark.

---

### Finding: re:/fwd: trick is now a confirmed spam signal
**Confidence:** CONFIRMED
**Evidence:** Multiple 2024-2025 deliverability sources (Mailforge, Buzzlead, Warmforge, Clay) — accessed 2026-02-06

- Gmail and other providers actively detect deceptive prefixes.
- Causes spam complaints, damages sender reputation.
- Gmail AI-driven spam filters (2025 updates) specifically flag these.

**Implications:** Never use re: or fwd: in cold email subject lines. Tactic is dead.

---

### Finding: Emojis are risky in B2B cold outbound — data leans negative
**Confidence:** INFERRED
**Evidence:** Reply.io, Lavender, Campaign Monitor, Lemlist — accessed 2026-02-06

- Reply.io: Emojis cause 6% lower open rates in cold email.
- Lavender warns against playful emojis — fits outside vendor email pattern.
- Most positive emoji data comes from opt-in marketing email, not cold outbound.

**Implications:** Avoid emojis in B2B cold outbound subject lines. They signal "marketing blast."

---

### Finding: Timeline hooks outperform problem hooks by 2.3x in reply rates
**Confidence:** CONFIRMED
**Evidence:** Digital Bloom 2025 benchmarks — accessed 2026-02-06

- Timeline hooks: 10.01% reply rate, 2.34% meeting rate
- Problem hooks: 4.39% reply rate, 0.69% meeting rate
- Numbers hooks also strong: 9.05% reply for CEO/Founder
- Explanation: Decision-makers want velocity/feasibility evidence, not problem validation they already know.

**Implications:** Frame hooks around timelines and compressed achievement ("cut onboarding from 6 weeks to 10 days") rather than problem statements ("struggling with slow onboarding?").

---

### Finding: "I noticed..." opener is overused but still effective with specific, researched observations
**Confidence:** INFERRED
**Evidence:** Josh Braun, Kyle Coleman, Jason Bay, Becc Holland — accessed 2026-02-06

- Josh Braun: First sentence shows homework done. Specific example tied to company.
- Becc Holland: Pattern interruption means avoiding typical sales email words. 49+ words/phrases compiled that undermine pattern interruption.
- Generic "I noticed" without specifics is flagged as AI-generated boilerplate.
- Observation must be genuinely specific — recent hire, product launch, LinkedIn post, job listing.

**Implications:** "I noticed..." is valid only with genuinely specific, recent observations. Better: lead with observation directly without "I noticed" prefix.

---

### Finding: Preview text (first line visible in inbox) is functionally part of the subject line
**Confidence:** CONFIRMED
**Evidence:** Kyle Coleman, Lavender, Jason Bay, Sam Nelson — accessed 2026-02-06

- Kyle Coleman: 1-3 word subject lines allow ~18 words of body preview to show, maximizing real estate.
- Lavender: Subject line's job is "not to get deleted." First body line does the real work of earning the open.
- Jason Bay: First line should be personalized and relevant — shows up in prospect's email client preview.
- Sam Nelson: Vast majority of sequence power is in first email. Personalize the first line.

**Implications:** Think of subject + first line as combined unit. Short subject creates space for body preview. First line is where personalization belongs.
