### 1.1 Voice Definition Frameworks (Generic B2B)

**Source:** [voice-definition-frameworks.md](https://github.com/inkeep/team-skills/blob/main/plugins/gtm/reports/Blog%20Content/b2b-saas-blog-writing-best-practices/evidence/voice-definition-frameworks.md)

#### Eight Voice Definition Frameworks

| Framework | Core Idea | Best For |
|-----------|-----------|----------|
| **Voice & Tone Model** | Voice is constant personality; tone varies by context | Foundation for any voice system |
| **Brand Archetypes** (Jung) | 12 personality archetypes mapped to brand motivation | Strategic positioning |
| **Voice Attribute Matrix** | 3-column chart: trait, description, do/don't | Day-to-day content reference |
| **Base Notes / Grace Notes** (Kessler) | Constant base traits + variable accent traits | B2B content teams |
| **"This But Not That"** (Mailchimp) | Contrasting pairs defining boundaries | Immediate personality clarity |
| **BrandSort** (Bloomstein) | Card sort → hierarchical communication goals | Workshop facilitation |
| **Four Dimensions** (NNGroup) | Four measurable spectrums | Research-backed calibration |
| **Personality Sliders** (GV Sprint) | Spectrum positioning in 3-hour sprint | Fast startup alignment |

#### "This But Not That" Examples (Industry Reference)

**Mailchimp:**
- Fun but not childish
- Clever but not silly
- Confident but not cocky
- Smart but not stodgy
- Cool but not alienating
- Informal but not sloppy
- Helpful but not overbearing

**Slack:**
- Confident but never cocky
- Witty but never silly
- Conversational but always appropriate
- Friendly but not ingratiating
- Helpful never overbearing

#### Base Notes / Grace Notes (Kessler Framework)

**Base notes (constant):** 3-4 unchanging personality traits
**Grace notes (variable):** Adjustable tonal qualities that flex by content type

Example base notes: Open, Honest, Passionate, Authoritative
Example grace notes: Witty, Edgy, Statesmanlike, Breathless, Angry

### 1.2 Tone Guidelines (Generic B2B)

**Source:** [voice-tone-openings-closings.md](https://github.com/inkeep/team-skills/blob/main/plugins/gtm/reports/Blog%20Content/b2b-saas-blog-writing-best-practices/evidence/voice-tone-openings-closings.md)
**Context:** Created February 2026 for blog content standards.

#### Doug Kessler's "Three Cs"

- **Clear** — No jargon unless audience uses it daily
- **Confident** — State claims directly; qualify only when genuinely uncertain
- **Compelling** — Authority comes from what you say, not formality

#### Tone Mapping by Content Type

| Content Type | Tone Position |
|--------------|---------------|
| Thought leadership | Confident, opinionated, forward-looking |
| How-to / tutorial | Helpful, clear, peer-to-peer |
| Case study | Professional, evidence-driven, restrained |
| Product update | Enthusiastic but factual, brief |
| Industry analysis | Measured, analytical, balanced |

#### Six B2B Tone Traps to Avoid

1. **Corporate jargon** — 88% of buyers say it erodes credibility
2. **Passive voice** — Obscures accountability, reduces clarity
3. **Hedging language** — "We believe that perhaps this could potentially..."
4. **Marketing speak / hype** — "Revolutionary," "game-changing," "best-in-class"
5. **Being too formal** — Stiff, academic prose creates distance
6. **Being too casual** — Overuse of slang undermines credibility

### 2.1 Pain Pillar Overview

**Source:** [inkeep-painpoint-messaging/REPORT.md](https://github.com/inkeep/team-skills/blob/main/plugins/gtm/reports/inkeep-painpoint-messaging/REPORT.md)
**Context:** Created February 2026 to map CEO pain point hypotheses to B2B persona archetypes for cold outreach messaging.

#### Pain Point 1: Fragmented AI Solutions

> *"Technical B2B SaaS companies are aiming to adopt AI to accelerate and improve every step of the customer journey. To do so, they are adopting a mix of AI solutions to cover different company-specific workflows and use cases covering everything from product onboarding, support engineering, success, and content teams. The problem: system of record platforms like support platforms often have siloed "closed garden" AI functionality that just doesn't work that well or doesn't connect or interface with a company's knowledge sources, customer data, 1st party product APIs and apps, or the other 3rd party SaaS solutions that the customer may use. Companies find themselves purchasing piece-meal solutions from specialized vendors (often startups) that solve narrow scenarios (e.g. "support chatbot") or workflows ("turn support tickets into documentation updates"). These solutions don't talk to each other, are configured and behave differently, or just don't quite work well in the specific requirements, data sources, workflows, or use cases unique to a company (and every company has a unique combination of these). Any limitation turns into a feature request to 1 vendor which may or may not get prioritized, and you're bottlenecked by that vendor's ability to ship features or make their solution extensible (which is often not a priority). This piecemeal approach poses significant burden on vendor procurement, security and governance, and actual adoption across every CX-related function of the company."*
> — Nick Gomez, CEO

**Core Problem:** Piecemeal AI solutions that don't integrate with each other, knowledge sources, customer data, or 1st-party APIs. Closed-garden platforms don't connect to external systems.

**Key Statistics:**

| Stat | Source |
|------|--------|
| 106 SaaS apps average per enterprise | Zylo |
| 95% cite integration as #1 AI adoption barrier | MuleSoft |
| 81% of CX leaders want single-system consolidation | Zendesk |
| 42% higher security incidents with 10+ AI tools | IBM |

**Inkeep Answer:** One platform that works with ANY help desk, pulls from ANY knowledge source, connects to 1st-party product APIs, and provides multi-agent orchestration vs single-agent ceiling.

---

#### Pain Point 2: Support Engineers Don't Get to Spend Time on Tough Problems

> *"In B2B SaaS companies, support is part of the product. B2B SaaS companies gravitate towards Enterprise sales motions, so providing a great customer experience and keeping individual customers happy is often a basic expectation of any of their Enterprise customers. Similarly, for product-led companies, support and a great user experience is imperative to promoting word of mouth and organic growth. Because of these dynamics, B2B SaaS companies invest heavily and see as a requirement to deliver timely, accurate, top-notch support. Delivering this support often relies on highly specialized talent including dedicated support engineers or engineering members of their product team. These engineers have to become always-up-to-date product experts (which has a ramp up period and is hard to hire for), but also need to spend significant amount of time performing **workflows** to help gather all the right context to resolve a problem. This often includes gathering additional context from the user, looking up customer information in downstream systems like error logs, CRMs, 1st party product-specific data sources or APIs, cross-referencing information with engineering and product backlogs, etc. All of these systems are often varied, and the workflows that stem from them are often custom to the company and how it operates. AI Support solutions today often **don't** cover anything beyond product knowledge chat Q&A, and even there, often don't handle the varied knowledge sources of a technical B2B SaaS company (knowledge base, docs, github, internal KBs like confluence, marketing site, etc.). The result: technical B2B SaaS companies struggle to adopt AI in a meaningful way beyond chat Q&A; keeping time-to-resolution stats high and support and product engineering teams swamped with the more manual and tedious parts of support."*
> — Nick Gomez, CEO

**Core Problem:** Support engineers spend time on context-gathering workflows rather than solving complex problems. AI solutions only cover basic chat Q&A.

**Key Statistics:**

| Stat | Source |
|------|--------|
| 49% of time answering repetitive questions | Zendesk |
| 50% of day lost to tool switching | Gartner |
| 20-30% deflection in technical B2B vs 50-70% consumer | Industry benchmark |
| 82% of support employees at burnout risk | Gallup |

**Inkeep Answer:** AI that goes beyond chat Q&A to handle context-gathering workflows, connects to varied knowledge sources, and works inside existing tools. 48-80% ticket deflection that actually works for technical B2B.

### 6.1 Implicit Beliefs (Extracted from Pain Points)

**Source:** [inkeep-painpoint-messaging/REPORT.md](https://github.com/inkeep/team-skills/blob/main/plugins/gtm/reports/inkeep-painpoint-messaging/REPORT.md)

#### Belief 1: Piecemeal AI Creates More Problems Than It Solves

**The take:** Technical B2B SaaS companies adopting fragmented AI solutions end up with vendor sprawl, security burden, and adoption friction. The "best-of-breed" approach to AI tooling fails because:
- Solutions don't talk to each other
- Each vendor has different configuration
- You're bottlenecked by vendor roadmaps
- Security/governance overhead multiplies

**Implied position:** One platform > multiple point solutions

---

#### Belief 2: AI Should Handle Workflows, Not Just Q&A

**The take:** Most AI support solutions only cover basic chat Q&A. This leaves 80% of the support engineer's job untouched. Real AI value comes from:
- Context-gathering workflows (error logs, CRMs, product APIs)
- Cross-referencing information across systems
- Taking actions, not just answering questions

**Implied position:** Agentic AI > chatbot AI

---

#### Belief 3: Technical B2B Is Different from Consumer

**The take:** Consumer AI achieves 50-70% deflection. Technical B2B only achieves 20-30%. The gap exists because:
- Technical documentation is more complex
- Knowledge is scattered across docs, GitHub, Confluence, Slack
- Every company has unique workflows and data sources

**Implied position:** Technical B2B needs purpose-built solutions, not consumer-focused tools

---

### 6.2 Implicit Beliefs (Extracted from Product Positioning)

**Source:** [inkeep-product-intelligence/REPORT.md](https://github.com/inkeep/team-skills/blob/main/plugins/gtm/reports/inkeep-product-intelligence/REPORT.md)

#### Belief 4: Multi-Agent > Single-Agent

**The take:** Single agents degrade beyond 5-10 tools. Specialized agents with discrete mandates resolve context complexity and conflicting instructions.

**Technical backing:** Graph-based orchestration with specialist routing, handoff patterns, and A2A communication.

---

#### Belief 5: Code + No-Code Should Work Together

**The take:** Engineers need SDK control. Business teams need visual builders. But they shouldn't be siloed. Full 2-way sync means changes in either environment reflect in the other.

**Why this matters:** Technical and non-technical teams can collaborate on the same agents without stepping on each other.

---

#### Belief 6: Self-Hosting Should Be an Option

**The take:** Cloud-only isn't acceptable for regulated industries or companies with strict data residency requirements. Full self-hosted deployment should be available.

**Competitive differentiation:** Most competitors are cloud-only.

---

#### Belief 7: Standards Over Lock-In

**The take:** MCP (Model Context Protocol) adoption eliminates the M x N integration problem and enables ecosystem evolution without vendor lock-in.

**Technical backing:** OpenAI-compatible APIs, OpenTelemetry for observability, MCP for tool communication.
